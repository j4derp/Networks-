Wireless Networking

Introduction

In addition to copper wires and fiber optic cables, networks can also transmit data using radio waves. This technology is called wireless networking (Wi-Fi).

Wireless Communication

Wireless networks send data through the air using radio waves. These radio waves are referred to as an unbounded medium because they are not encased in a sheath like a fiber optic cable or unshielded twisted pair (UTP) cables. This means that the wireless signal can reach areas that a wired network cannot.
Wireless networks use specific radio frequency ranges that are regulated by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC). The two most common frequencies used by wireless networks are the 2.4 GHZ and 5 GHz ranges.

  • 2.4 GHz wireless signals are wider, but shorter. This means they can travel further, but transmit data at a slower rate.
  • 5 GHz wireless signals are thinner, but taller. This means they do not travel as far, but can transmit data at a faster rate.

Hardware Needed

To build and maintain a wireless network, you typically need:

  • Wireless Access Points (WAPs): Devices that broadcast the wireless signal.
  • Wireless Routers: Combine routing and wireless access functions.
  • Wireless Network Interface Cards (NICs): Allow client devices to connect to the wireless network.
  • Antennas: Internal or external, used to extend or direct signal coverage.
  • RADIUS Server (for enterprise networks): Manages authentication, authorization, and accounting.
  • Firewalls: Provide protection against unauthorized access.
  • Switches and Ethernet Cabling (optional): For connecting WAPs in larger or enterprise setups.

Pros and Cons

Pros Cons
Mobility – Users can move freely while staying connected Signal Interference – Other devices or walls can degrade performance
Easy Installation – No need for extensive cabling Lower Speed/Reliability – Typically slower and less stable than wired
Scalability – Easier to expand coverage by adding access points Security Risks – Susceptible to eavesdropping if not properly secured
Flexibility – Ideal for locations where wiring is impractical Range Limitations – Performance drops significantly over distance
Cost Savings – Reduces need for physical infrastructure Shared Medium – Multiple users on the same channel can cause congestion
