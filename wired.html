Wired Network

Introduction

A wired network, also known as a Local Area Network (LAN), uses physical cables to connect devices, like computers, printers, and servers, within a specific area (e.g., a home or office).
These connections are typically established using Ethernet cables, which plug into a router, switch, or another network device.

Hardware Needed

To build and maintain a wireless network, you typically need:

  • Network Interface Cards (NICs) These are internal components in computers that enable them to connect to the network.
  • Ethernet Cables: These cables are used to physically connect devices to the network.
  • Router: This device acts as the central hub, routing data between devices and potentially connecting to the internet.
  • Modem: If the network needs to connect to the internet, a modem will be required.
  • Switch: This device expands the network by allowing more devices to connect to it.
  • Access Point: If you want to connect wireless devices to your wired network, an access point will be needed.

Pros and Cons

Pros Cons
Speed/Reliability – Faster, consistent data transfer Limited Mobility – Devices restricted by cables
Security – Harder to intercept without physical access Installation Costs – Expensive cabling and hardware
Stability – Reliable in high-density environments Scalability Issues – Complex, costly to expand
Low Interference – Unaffected by devices or obstacles Trip Hazards – Cables can be dangerous if unmanaged
